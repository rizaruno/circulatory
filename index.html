Every heartbeat
is a loop.

Your circulatory system moves blood through your body about 100,000 times a day — nonstop, in exactly the same order, every single time. That's not just biology. It's an algorithm. Explore it the way a computer scientist would: by breaking it down, spotting patterns, simplifying the map, and tracing the steps.

Pillar 02 · Algorithms

The double-circulation algorithm

An algorithm is a precise, ordered sequence of steps — do this, then this, then this, in exactly that order, every time. Your blood follows one. Pillar 04 · Abstraction

Simplifying the map

Every diagram on this page has already been an abstraction — real blood vessels don't look like clean curved pipes, and a real map of your vessels would need over 60,000 miles of lines to draw. Abstraction means keeping only the details that matter for the task, and hiding the rest.

A subway map does this too

A subway map isn't geographically accurate — the curves are straightened and the distances are stretched. It hides everything except stations and lines, because that's all a rider actually needs.
